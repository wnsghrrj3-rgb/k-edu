In the 1960s, a psychologist discovered that children who could resist eating a marshmallow for fifteen minutes in exchange for a second one tended to perform better academically and socially years later. This famous experiment suggested that the ability to delay gratification — to wait for a bigger reward rather than grabbing an immediate one — is a powerful predictor of success. Self-control, it seems, matters more than intelligence in shaping long-term outcomes.
