📖 Passage A — Neuroplasticity
For centuries, scientists believed that the adult brain was essentially fixed — unable to grow new connections or repair itself after injury. This view has been overturned by the discovery of neuroplasticity, the brain's remarkable ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural pathways throughout life. When a person learns a new skill, such as playing the piano, the brain physically changes: neurons form new connections, and frequently used pathways become stronger. Conversely, pathways that are rarely used gradually weaken. This "use it or lose it" principle has profound implications for education. It suggests that intelligence is not a fixed trait determined at birth, but rather a capacity that can be developed through sustained effort and practice. Students who believe their abilities can grow — what psychologist Carol Dweck calls a "growth mindset" — tend to achieve more than those who view intelligence as unchangeable.

📖 Passage B — AI and Bias
Artificial intelligence systems are often presented as objective decision-makers, free from the biases that affect human judgment. However, this perception is misleading. AI systems learn from data — and if that data reflects existing social biases, the AI will reproduce and even amplify those biases. For example, a hiring algorithm trained on historical data from a company that predominantly hired men may learn to systematically disadvantage female applicants. The danger lies not in the technology itself, but in the uncritical assumption that automated systems are inherently fair. Addressing AI bias requires diverse development teams, transparent algorithms, and ongoing auditing of outcomes to ensure that these powerful tools serve all members of society equitably.

📖 Passage C — Climate Feedback Loops
①Climate change is often described as a gradual process, but scientists warn that certain feedback loops could trigger rapid, irreversible changes. ②One well-documented example is the ice-albedo feedback: as global temperatures rise, polar ice melts, exposing darker ocean water beneath. ③This darker surface absorbs more solar radiation instead of reflecting it, which causes further warming and more ice loss — a self-reinforcing cycle. ④Similarly, thawing permafrost releases stored methane, a greenhouse gas far more potent than carbon dioxide, which accelerates warming even further. ⑤These feedback mechanisms illustrate why climate scientists emphasize the urgency of reducing emissions now, before such cycles become irreversible.
