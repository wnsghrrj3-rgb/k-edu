📖 Passage A — Culture and Identity
Culture is not simply a set of traditions passed down from generation to generation. It is a dynamic process through which individuals actively construct their sense of self. When people move to a new country, they do not simply abandon their original culture and adopt a new one. Instead, they engage in a process of cultural negotiation, blending elements from both cultures to create a unique identity. This hybrid identity is not a sign of confusion or weakness; rather, it reflects the remarkable human capacity to adapt and grow. Research in social psychology suggests that individuals with bicultural identities often demonstrate greater cognitive flexibility and creativity than those who identify with only one culture.

📖 Passage B — The Paradox of Choice
In modern consumer societies, people are bombarded with an overwhelming number of choices in every aspect of life — from breakfast cereals to career paths. Conventional wisdom holds that more options lead to greater satisfaction. However, psychologist Barry Schwartz argues that an abundance of choices can actually paralyze decision-making and increase anxiety. When faced with dozens of options, people tend to experience "analysis paralysis," spending excessive time comparing alternatives and ultimately feeling less confident about their final decision. Moreover, having many choices raises expectations — if there are fifty options, surely the perfect one must exist. This leads to chronic dissatisfaction, as the chosen option inevitably falls short of an idealized alternative that exists only in the imagination.

📖 Passage C — The Purpose of Education
①Education in the 21st century faces a fundamental question: should schools primarily prepare students for the job market, or should they cultivate well-rounded individuals capable of critical thinking? ②Those who favor a pragmatic approach argue that education must equip students with marketable skills to compete in a global economy. ③However, critics of this view contend that reducing education to job training neglects the development of ethical reasoning, civic responsibility, and creative expression. ④Perhaps the most balanced perspective is that education should serve both purposes — preparing students for careers while also nurturing the intellectual curiosity and moral judgment that define an engaged citizen.
