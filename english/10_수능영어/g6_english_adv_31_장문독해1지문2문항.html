📖 장문 A (Part 1/2)
In 1928, Scottish scientist Alexander Fleming returned from vacation to find that a mold called Penicillium had contaminated one of his petri dishes. Rather than discarding the ruined experiment, he noticed something remarkable: the bacteria surrounding the mold had been destroyed. This accidental discovery would eventually lead to the development of penicillin, the world's first widely used antibiotic.
📖 장문 A (Part 2/2)
However, Fleming alone could not turn his observation into a usable drug. It took more than a decade and the combined efforts of Howard Florey and Ernst Boris Chain to develop a method for mass-producing penicillin. By 1944, enough penicillin was available to treat Allied soldiers wounded in World War II, saving countless lives. The story of penicillin reminds us that groundbreaking discoveries often require both a spark of curiosity and years of collaborative effort to reach their full potential.

📖 장문 B (Part 1/2)
The concept of "flow" was introduced by psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi to describe a state of complete absorption in an activity. During flow, people lose track of time, feel deeply focused, and experience a sense of effortless control. Athletes call it "being in the zone," musicians describe it as "the music playing itself," and writers speak of words "flowing" onto the page.
📖 장문 B (Part 2/2)
Csikszentmihalyi found that flow is most likely to occur when a task is challenging enough to require full attention but not so difficult as to cause anxiety. If the challenge exceeds one's skill level, stress results; if it falls too far below, boredom sets in. The sweet spot — where challenge and skill are perfectly matched — is where flow lives. This insight has been applied in education, workplace design, and game development to create environments that promote deep engagement.
