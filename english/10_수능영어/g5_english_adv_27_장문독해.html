In 2016, a team of scientists discovered a new species of frog in the mountains of Ecuador. The frog, no bigger than a thumbnail, had transparent skin that allowed researchers to see its beating heart. They named it the "glass frog" because of this remarkable feature.

What surprised the scientists most was the frog's defense mechanism. When threatened, the glass frog could redirect most of its red blood cells into its liver, making its body almost completely transparent. This camouflage was so effective that predators would look right through the frog without noticing it.

The discovery raised new questions about how animals evolve to survive. If a tiny frog could develop such an advanced ability, what other hidden tricks might nature still be hiding from us?

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is a massive collection of floating plastic waste in the North Pacific Ocean. It covers an area roughly three times the size of France and contains an estimated 80,000 tons of plastic.

Most of this plastic does not biodegrade. Instead, it breaks into tiny pieces called microplastics, which are eaten by fish, sea turtles, and birds. These microplastics enter the food chain and eventually reach humans who eat seafood.

Several organizations are now working to clean up the patch using advanced technology, including floating barriers that collect plastic without harming marine life. However, experts warn that cleanup alone is not enough — reducing plastic production and improving recycling are equally important.
